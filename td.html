Key definition

Technical drawing (also called board practice) is the special language used by engineers and architects to show objects, symbols, lines, and curves on paper.

Drawing Instruments

Drawing instruments are tools used in technical drawing. They do not finish with use and can last a long time if properly cared for. They include:

  • 1. Drawing Board – A flat, smooth wooden board used for placing drawing paper.
  • 2. Tee-square – A T-shaped tool used for drawing horizontal lines and fixing paper on the board.
  • 3. Set-square – A right-angled triangle (45° or 30°/60°) made of plastic, used for vertical and slanted lines.
  • 4. Ruler – A 30 cm ruler used for measuring and drawing straight lines.
  • 5. Protractor – A half-circle plastic tool used for measuring angles.
  • 6. French Curves – Used for drawing different kinds of curves.
  • 7. Drawing Set – A pack containing tools like a compass and divider.

Drawing Materials

Drawing materials are items that get finished when used. They include:

  • 1. Drawing Paper – The common one is A3 size, but we also have A1, A2, A4, etc.
  • 2. Pencils – 2H for construction lines, H for lettering and thick lines, HB for freehand sketching. Pencils should be sharpened to a cone shape.
  • 3. Pencil Sharpener – Used to sharpen pencils.
  • 4. Emery Cloth – Used to make pencil tips sharp without cutting.
  • 5. Brush – Used to clean dirt from paper after erasing.
  • 6. Eraser – A soft rubber used to remove mistakes.

Care of Drawing Instruments

  • Do not use sharp objects on the drawing board or set-squares.
  • Cover the board when not in use.
  • Don’t play with the Tee-square.
  • Keep instruments in their packs when not in use.
  • Keep drawing paper flat in a file.

Board Practice Activities

  • 1. Setting Paper on the Board
    • Place A3 paper on the board.
    • Put the Tee-square on the left side to align the paper.
    • Adjust the paper until straight.
    • Fix with masking tape or clips.
  • 2. Drawing Border Line
    • Border lines are drawn 10–15 mm from the paper edge.
    • Use compass, Tee-square, and set-square to draw straight border lines around the paper.
  • 3. Drawing the Title Block
    • Draw a rectangle (40/50 mm × 100/120 mm) at the bottom right corner.
    • Divide into 5 equal parts.
    • Title block should contain: Name, School, Title, Scale, Date.

Freehand Sketching

Freehand sketching means drawing quickly with only a pencil, eraser, and hand (no instruments).

Techniques:

  • 1. Straight Lines – Mark two points and draw across them.
  • 2. Curves – Mark several dots, then join them smoothly.
  • 3. Circles – Draw a square, divide it into four, and join the arcs to form a circle.
  • 4. Ellipse – Draw a rectangle, divide into four, and join arcs to form an ellipse.

LINES

A line is a long narrow mark that joins two points. It has length but no thickness. Lines may be straight, curved, or crooked.

Types of Straight Lines:

  • Horizontal line
  • • Vertical line
  • • Inclined line
  • • Parallel line
  • • Perpendicular line
  • • Transversal line

Types and Uses of Lines

  • 1. Thick Continuous Line – For visible outlines and borders.
  • 2. Thin Continuous Line – For construction and dimension lines.
  • 3. Thick Long Chain Line – For viewing and cutting planes.
  • 4. Thin Long Chain Line – For center lines and pitch circles.
  • 5. Thin Continuous Line with Arrows – For dimensioning.
  • 6. Thin Short Dashes – For hidden details.
  • 7. Thin Wavy Line – For limits of partial views.
  • 8. Thick Wavy Line – For irregular boundaries.
  • 9. Thin Zig-Zag Line – For long breaks.

Bisection of Lines

To bisect means to divide into two equal parts.

Steps:

  • 1. Draw a line AB.
  • 2. With compass at A, draw two arcs above and below the line.
  • 3. Repeat from B, using same radius, to cut the first arcs at C and D.
  • 4. Draw line CD. It will cut AB into two equal parts.

Division of Lines

  • 1. Into Equal Parts – Draw a slant line, mark equal points, and draw parallels to divide the main line.
  • 2. Into Proportion – Use ratio markings on a slant line (e.g. 3:6:2) and join to divide main line accordingly.

ANGLES

An angle is formed when two lines meet at a point. Angles are measured in degrees using a protractor.

Types of Angles

  • 1. Right Angle – 90°
  • 2. Acute Angle – Less than 90°
  • 3. Obtuse Angle – Between 90° and 180°
  • 4. Straight Angle – 180°
  • 5. Reflex Angle – Between 180° and 360°
  • 6. Complementary Angles – Two angles that add up to 90°
  • 7. Supplementary Angles – Two angles that add up to 180°

Construction of Angles

Angles can be constructed using:

  • 1. Set-square
  • 2. Two set-squares together
  • 3. Protractor
  • 4. Pair of compasses

Evaluation Questions

  • 1. What is a line?
  • 2. Mention five types of lines in technical drawing.
  • 3. Draw and bisect a horizontal line 7 cm long.
  • 4. Draw a horizontal line 9 cm long and divide it into 6 equal parts.
  • 5. Define an angle.
  • 6. Name and describe five types of angles.
